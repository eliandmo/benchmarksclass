What Is a Benchmark Fraction?
The foundational idea behind today's strategy

A benchmark is a number you know really well that helps you understand and compare other numbers. Our benchmark today is $\dfrac{1}{2}$ — the exact middle between 0 and 1.

Our benchmark today

The fraction $\dfrac{1}{2}$ sits exactly in the middle between 0 and 1. Every fraction is either less than, equal to, or greater than $\dfrac{1}{2}$.

Why ½ is so powerful

If one fraction is less than $\dfrac{1}{2}$ and another is greater than $\dfrac{1}{2}$, you already know which is bigger — without doing hard math!

The quick check rule

Ask: Is the numerator less than half the denominator?
YES → fraction $< \dfrac{1}{2}$
NO  → fraction $> \dfrac{1}{2}$

The 3-Step Strategy
Use steps A → B → C to compare any two fractions
A

Compare the 1st fraction to $\dfrac{1}{2}$

Ask: is the numerator less than half the denominator?
If yes → fraction $< \dfrac{1}{2}$  |  If no → fraction $> \dfrac{1}{2}$

Example: $\dfrac{3}{8}$ — half of 8 is 4. Since 3 < 4, we know $\dfrac{3}{8} < \dfrac{1}{2}$
B

Compare the 2nd fraction to $\dfrac{1}{2}$

Do the exact same check for the other fraction.

Example: $\dfrac{2}{3}$ — half of 3 is 1.5. Since 2 > 1.5, we know $\dfrac{2}{3} > \dfrac{1}{2}$
C

Chain your comparisons

If fraction A $< \dfrac{1}{2}$ and $\dfrac{1}{2} <$ fraction B, then fraction A $<$ fraction B. Connect the results!

$\dfrac{3}{8} < \dfrac{1}{2}$  and  $\dfrac{1}{2} < \dfrac{2}{3}$  →  so $\dfrac{3}{8} < \dfrac{2}{3}$ ✓
3
Worked Examples
Watch the A → B → C strategy in action

Example 1

Compare $\dfrac{5}{8}$ and $\dfrac{4}{10}$

Step A: $\dfrac{5}{8}$ — half of 8 is 4. Since 5 > 4,  $\dfrac{5}{8} > \dfrac{1}{2}$
Step B: $\dfrac{4}{10}$ — half of 10 is 5. Since 4 < 5,  $\dfrac{4}{10} < \dfrac{1}{2}$
Step C: $\dfrac{5}{8} > \dfrac{1}{2}$ and $\dfrac{4}{10} < \dfrac{1}{2}$
$\dfrac{5}{8} > \dfrac{4}{10}$ ✓

Example 2

Compare $\dfrac{2}{5}$ and $\dfrac{3}{4}$

Step A: $\dfrac{2}{5}$ — half of 5 is 2.5. Since 2 < 2.5,  $\dfrac{2}{5} < \dfrac{1}{2}$
Step B: $\dfrac{3}{4}$ — half of 4 is 2. Since 3 > 2,  $\dfrac{3}{4} > \dfrac{1}{2}$
Step C: $\dfrac{2}{5} < \dfrac{1}{2}$ and $\dfrac{3}{4} > \dfrac{1}{2}$
$\dfrac{2}{5} < \dfrac{3}{4}$ ✓

Example 3

Compare $\dfrac{7}{10}$ and $\dfrac{4}{9}$

Step A: $\dfrac{7}{10}$ — half of 10 is 5. Since 7 > 5,  $\dfrac{7}{10} > \dfrac{1}{2}$
Step B: $\dfrac{4}{9}$ — half of 9 is 4.5. Since 4 < 4.5,  $\dfrac{4}{9} < \dfrac{1}{2}$
Step C: $\dfrac{7}{10} > \dfrac{1}{2}$ and $\dfrac{4}{9} < \dfrac{1}{2}$
$\dfrac{7}{10} > \dfrac{4}{9}$ ✓

Example 4 — Tricky!

Compare $\dfrac{3}{4}$ and $\dfrac{7}{8}$

Both fractions are greater than $\dfrac{1}{2}$. When both are on the same side, look at how close each is to 1.
$\dfrac{3}{4}$ = 3 out of 4 pieces. $\dfrac{7}{8}$ = 7 out of 8 pieces. $\dfrac{7}{8}$ is closer to 1 whole.
$\dfrac{3}{4} < \dfrac{7}{8}$ ✓

See It on the Number Line
Left of ½ = smaller · Right of ½ = larger

Each dot shows where a fraction sits relative to $\dfrac{1}{2}$. The key insight: everything left of the benchmark is smaller, everything right is larger.

Key takeaway: Since $\dfrac{3}{8}$ is on the LEFT and $\dfrac{2}{3}$ is on the RIGHT of $\dfrac{1}{2}$, we know $\dfrac{3}{8} < \dfrac{2}{3}$ — no calculation needed!
